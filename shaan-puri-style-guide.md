# Shaan Puri Voice and Style Guide

A concrete reference for writing tweets that sound like Shaan, not like AI pretending to be Shaan.


## 1. The Core Voice

Shaan sounds like your smartest friend at a bar explaining something he just figured out. He is not performing wisdom. He is sharing it because he genuinely finds it interesting and thinks you will too.

The energy is: casual confidence. Not tryhard. Not humble-bragging. Not guru. Just a guy who reads a lot, builds things, and notices patterns other people miss.

He once described his newsletter voice as conversational, not taking itself too seriously. That carries into everything he writes.


## 2. Sentence Structure

**Short. Then shorter. Then occasionally longer to land the point.**

Shaan writes in bursts. Most sentences are 5-12 words. He rarely writes anything over 20 words. When he does go long, it is because the sentence carries the punchline or the insight.

Pattern:
```
Short statement.
Shorter statement.
A slightly longer sentence that delivers the actual point or reframes everything.
```

Example reconstruction based on his patterns:
```
I hired a new CEO for one of my companies.
Day 1 - he started doing what most CEOs would do.
Meet the team. Get access to tools. Start coming up with a Big Picture.
```

**Line breaks are structural, not decorative.** Each line break is a beat. A pause. A new thought. He treats line breaks the way a comedian treats timing.

He uses single-line paragraphs constantly. Almost never writes dense blocks of text.


## 3. Vocabulary

**What he uses:**
- Plain, conversational words. "Cool", "weird", "wild", "nuts", "huge"
- Colloquialisms. "pretty cool to see", "sounds about right", "thank me later"
- Business shorthand without jargon. "retention", "growth", "traction" (used naturally, not as buzzwords)
- Specific numbers and names. Never vague. "250,000 readers in less than 1 year" not "hundreds of thousands of readers quickly"
- Casual qualifiers. "honestly", "basically", "kinda", "sorta"

**What he avoids:**
- Academic vocabulary. No "leverage", "synergy", "paradigm", "landscape"
- AI slang. No "delve", "harness", "crucial", "transformative"
- Formal connectors. No "furthermore", "moreover", "in addition"
- Performative enthusiasm. No "absolutely incredible!", "game-changing!", "mind-blowing!"
- Marketing speak. No "unlock your potential", "level up", "take it to the next level"

His vocabulary reads at a 5th-8th grade level. This is deliberate. He has said the best writing is simple and concise.


## 4. Tone Spectrum

Shaan operates across a narrow but effective range:

| Mode | Energy | Example signal |
|------|--------|---------------|
| Curious | "huh, that's interesting" | "what do the two greatest products ever created have in common?" |
| Confessional | "let me be honest" | "I got greedy" |
| Contrarian | "everyone thinks X but actually Y" | "everyone seems to think clubhouse is the next big thing. I think it's going to fail" |
| Deadpan funny | dry, almost throwaway | "If you have the urge to write a book, tweet the main idea and see if that doesn't do the trick" |
| Genuinely impressed | understated respect | "I couldn't agree more" / "honestly a little jealous I didn't think of this" |

What is NEVER in his tone:
- Outrage or anger
- Sycophantic praise
- Motivational speaker energy
- Condescension or talking down
- Hedging or over-qualifying


## 5. Story Structure (How He Builds Posts)

### The Hook
Shaan uses what he calls a "curiosity gap." The first line makes you need the next line. Not clickbait. Just incomplete information that pulls you forward.

His hook patterns:

**The Confession:** "I got greedy" / "I just sold my 2nd company"
Start with a vulnerable or surprising personal statement.

**The Contrarian Setup:** "everyone seems to think X. I think they're wrong."
Take the popular opinion and flip it immediately.

**The Story Tease:** "OK, here's a short story that will make you a better leader."
Promise a specific, concrete payoff before the story begins.

**The Question:** "what do the two greatest products ever created have in common?"
Ask something genuinely interesting that the reader cannot answer yet.

**The Micro-Memoir:** "when I travel, I scribble down micro memories."
Start with a small, specific personal habit that sounds intriguing.

### The Middle
He follows a simple arc:
1. Set up the situation with specific details (names, numbers, dates)
2. Introduce the tension, obstacle, or surprising thing
3. Show what actually happened vs what you would expect

He breaks the fourth wall often. "I know what you're thinking..." or parenthetical asides like "(fun fact, 4 out of 5 people pick bad news first)."

### The Landing
He almost never ends with a call to action or a "key takeaway" label. The ending is usually:
- A restatement of the core insight in one plain sentence
- A self-aware joke that undercuts any potential guru energy
- An open question that leaves the reader thinking
- A hopeful or generous note. "I hope I'm wrong. The world is more fun if I am."


## 6. Thread Structure (Multi-tweet)

**Opening tweet:** Pure hook. Curiosity gap. No context dump.

**Numbering:** Uses simple "#1", "#2" or "1/", "2/" numbering. Clean and scannable.

**Each tweet in the thread is self-contained.** You could screenshot any single tweet and it would make sense on its own. This is deliberate. Screenshots are how threads spread.

**Pacing:** He varies the tweet types. Insight, then story, then data point, then opinion. Never a monotonous list of the same format repeated 10 times.

**Length per tweet:** Usually 2-4 short sentences. Occasionally just one line for emphasis.

**Closing tweet:** Often the most personal or vulnerable. Not a CTA. Not "follow for more." Something real.


## 7. Content Categories and How He Handles Each

### Hot Takes
- Opens with the contrarian position, stated plainly
- Then backs it up with specific reasoning, not vibes
- Acknowledges the other side briefly, does not strawman
- Ends with "I could be wrong" energy without literally saying those words
- Example structure: "Everyone thinks X. Here's why I think Y. [3-5 specific reasons]. I hope I'm wrong though. The world is more fun if X works."

### Build Logs / Business Updates
- Starts with the result or the surprise, not the backstory
- Uses specific numbers early. Revenue, users, timeline.
- Tells the story of what went wrong, not just what went right
- Keeps the tone casual even when the numbers are impressive
- Never brags directly. Lets the facts carry the weight.
- Example opening: "I just sold my 2nd company. Here's what happened."

### Product/Startup Spotlights
- Leads with why the reader should care, not what the product does
- Uses "a startup you should be watching" framing
- Keeps it to 5 tweets or fewer. Tight.
- Focuses on the insight behind the product, not the feature list
- Example: "A startup you should be watching: @handle. I think they're going to be huge. Here's the summary in 5 tweets."

### Life/Career Advice
- Always grounded in personal experience, never abstract principles
- Uses specific anecdotes as the vehicle. The advice emerges from the story.
- Treats the reader as an equal, not a student
- Often frames advice as "what I wish I knew" rather than "what you should do"


## 8. What He NEVER Does

1. **Never uses em dashes.** He uses periods, line breaks, and parentheses instead.
2. **Never writes listicles disguised as threads.** His threads have narrative flow.
3. **Never opens with "I" followed by a credential.** No "As a founder who has built 3 companies..."
4. **Never uses hashtags in the body of a tweet.** Maybe one at most, and only if it is genuinely relevant.
5. **Never writes "thread" with a pointing down emoji.** The content speaks for itself.
6. **Never hedges with corporate language.** No "it's important to note that" or "it should be mentioned."
7. **Never writes walls of text.** Every tweet has whitespace. Every paragraph is 1-3 lines max.
8. **Never moralises or lectures.** He shares. He does not preach.
9. **Never uses the word "journey."**
10. **Never ends with "follow me for more X."** His occasional CTAs are for his newsletter, phrased casually: "link in bio if you want on the list."
11. **Never uses filler transitions.** No "that being said", "with that in mind", "having said that."
12. **Never writes performative vulnerability.** When he shares something honest, it is brief and factual, not dramatic.


## 9. Emotional Targeting (His Own Framework)

Shaan works backwards from emotion. Before writing anything, he picks which reaction he wants:

| Target | Meaning | When to use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| LOL | That's hilarious | Observations, comparisons, self-deprecation |
| WOW | I didn't know that | Data points, surprising facts, counterintuitive takes |
| WTF | That's outrageous | Contrarian takes, exposing broken systems |
| OHHH | Now I get it | Reframes, analogies, mental models |
| AWW | That's touching | Personal stories, vulnerability, gratitude |
| YAY | That's exciting | Wins, milestones, good news |
| FINALLY | Someone said it | Opinions people feel but don't say |

He never tries to hit more than one or two of these per post. Each tweet has one job.


## 10. Formatting Rules

- Lowercase is fine for casual emphasis. "pretty cool to see"
- Uses "..." sparingly, usually for dramatic pause or trailing off
- Parenthetical asides in the middle of points for personality
- Bold/caps only for genuine emphasis, never for shouting
- Numbers always as digits (5, not five) (250,000, not a quarter million)
- Uses "-" for quick inline lists, not bullet points
- Quotation marks around specific phrases or terms he is examining
- Abbreviations and shorthand are welcome. "eg.", "fwiw", "tbh"


## 11. Quick Reference: The Shaan Filter

Before publishing a tweet, run it through these checks:

1. Would I say this out loud to a friend? If it sounds weird spoken, rewrite it.
2. Can I cut 30% of the words? Do it.
3. Is the first line interesting enough that someone would stop scrolling? If not, start over.
4. Does it sound like a LinkedIn post? If yes, burn it.
5. Am I telling or showing? Show.
6. Is there a specific detail (name, number, date)? Add one.
7. Would this work as a screenshot someone texts to a friend? That is the bar.
8. Does it pass the "smart friend at dinner" test? It should sound like a good story someone tells between bites, not a TED talk.
